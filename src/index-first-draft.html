Magnitude is logarithmic. Each step is ~2.5. The bigger the magnitude number, the fainter the star. Very bright stars are negative. A object of magnitude 1 is 100 brighter than one of mangitude 6.

Time to travel back to earth to find some facts about the twinkling.
Back at earth let's look into the night sky again. Ah yes, that looks better. So something between here and the star is causing the twinkle effect. It's not the star itself.
Twinkling also "scintillation" is a generic term for variations in apparent brightness or position of a distant luminous object viewed through a medium
It's more or less the very same effect as you can see here on the ground of the pool. There a brighter and fainter areas of light.
To have such an effect you need to look at a far distant objects so they act like point sources of light. Stars are matching this definition. Our moon and our solar system planets are not far away enough so you can't see the effect.

✨ Size ✨

We just gathered a fact ✨ **SIZE** ✨ as meteroids are mostly pebble sized so are our meteors.

Definition

Meteors are leaving a bright trail as they are heated to incandescence (glowing) by the friction of the atmosphere and they are so small that they entirely burn up and disappear.
It's such a thin layer that the objects entering it burn up quickly or arrive at earth as meteorites after a short visible phase.
